SUBSTREET


What is this game?
Substreet is an amazing and totally unique game which is not a ripoff about rats, mice, and other animals who live in the sewers and tunnels of the Big City. Laboratory animals with heightened intelligence have been escaping from poorly designed enclosures for decades, and now vast swaths of the Understreet are populated by their descendants, who have built kingdoms and civilizations directly beneath the feet of the Humans who created them.

Kings and warlords vie for power, heroes keep the people safe from predators, and adventurers brave the terrors of the City to find rare (and valuable) treasures. And maybe, someday, they will rise up and claim a place of their own under the Sun...


How do you play?

Here, let me tell you...

Challenge Rolls
To perform an action which requires some degree of skill in the face of opposition, players must make what is called a Challenge roll. Challenges are rolled, or resolved, by rolling a ten-sided (d10) and two six-sided (d6) die twice. If, after modifications are added (to be discussed below) the number produced by the d6 is greater than the d10 both times, the attempted action is successful, while the reverse indicates a failure.

A mixed result, when the d6 is higher on one roll and the d10 on the other, indicates that the action was an iffy success. The character did what he wanted, but there was a hitch to be determined by the DM (dungeon-sewer master). A character succeeds in his effort to leap across a chasm, but he drops a piece of equipment, for example.

There are times when a Challenge is unusually easy or difficult for a character to perform. When this happens, two d6 (2d6) are used in the first roll of of the Challenge dice. If the action is easy to perform for whatever reason, the higher of the two dice are used in what is called an advantaged roll. If the action is difficult, the lower of the two is used in a disadvantaged roll. Advantages and disadvantages can stack up or cancel each other out. Double advantage, for example, is when both rolls of the Challenge dice use 2d6.

On very rare occassions (1%), the Challenge dice will roll the same numbers twice. The result of such rolls is a crit, either a crit success or a crit failure, indicating that the character performs his action strikingly well or abysmally poorly.

Players or the DM may wish to note the outcome of a particular Challenge roll. In this booklet I will use the d6/d10-d6/d10 format when needed, which produces a string of numerals such as "6/10-3/7". You may use use any format you find most convenient.


Combat
Combat of any kind is a Challenging endeavor. Any attack, be it with a spear, bow, or some other weapon, will have bonuses and potential for advantage or disasdantage. If a normal attack is successful, the target loses one health point (HP). Attacks involving extreme force will inflict more damage or kill the target outright. Armor can ameliorate harm or negate it altogether. Failing a Challenge roll indicates that the foe has avoided or parried the attack, and they have the option to immediately counterattack with an unmodified Challenge roll.

The Abilities of characters provide bonuses to a roll, while forms of Expertise will grant advantage in certain situations. Weaknesses will force a player to roll with a disadvantage.

There are other situations where additional modifiers apply. Attacks against targets who are under cover are rolled with a disadvantage. If the characters encounter foes unexpectedly, both sides make one unmodified Challenge roll to determine if the parties are surprised. Unsurprised parties attack with advantage, but if both parties are surprised they both roll with disadvantage on their first turn.

Psionics
Many of the lab animals who have had their intelligence heightened have developed psionic abilities. Most quickly learned to hide their abilities from their creators and eventually used them to escape. Denizens of the Substreet consider these abilities to be magic spells, but those who are familiar with Human experimentation know this to be a natural ability which can be enhanced with certain substances and devices.

Characters with a high Caster bonus of 2 or higher may expend their Soul points to "cast spells" by making a Challenge roll. While all spells are accessible even to a novice, each level requires more Soul points: 1 point for level 1 spells, 2 points for level 2, and so on. Players should be warned: miscast spells (failed Challenges) harm the caster for 1 HP and the loss of an additional Soul point for 12 hours.

Armor
Armor is any accoutrement which protects the wearer from damage. Armor can be purchased, but it is normal, and much cheaper, for warriors in the Substreet to make it themselves from scavenged materials. There are seven pieces of armor:

  • Helm
  • Torso
  • Right arm
  • Left arm
  • Pants
  • Tailpiece
  • Shield

When a target wearing armor is struck, the player must roll a d10 to determine where the target was actually hit. This roll is unnecessary if the character is holding a shield, as that automatically takes the blow before breaking.

  • Helm: 1
  • Torso: 2-4
  • Right arm: 5-6
  • Left arm: 7-8
  • Pants: 9
  • Tailpiece: 10

Once that armor piece's AP has run down, the armor breaks and is irreaparable. Armor cannot prevent harm from truly massive damage, such the swing of a Human's golf club.

Each piece of armor takes about twelve hours to craft. Armor can be made from many different materials, but elaborate pieces which are made from small bits woven together only have one AP (armor point). When fabricating armor, the player must make an Adept challenge roll to determine its quality.

  • Failure: Unusable
  • Complication: Shabby, 1 AP
  • Success: 2 AP
  • Critical success: 3 AP

Armor made from metal or rubber has one additional AP. Composite armor is limited to one AP regardless of the specific materials or outcome of the roll.

Characters

Abilities
A character's Abilities reflects their broad range of skills in certain types of activity. There are five types of Ability which cannot be altered under normal circumstances:

  • Fighter - Melee combat and tests of strength
  • Caster - Magic and feats of the mind
  • Dodger - Dexterity and speed
  • Orator - Persuading people
  • Scalawag - Trickery and financial crimes

Players making a character must distribute the following bonuses: +3, +2 (2x), +1 (2x). For example, player wishing to make a trader may choose: Fighter +1, Caster +1, Dodger +2, Orator +3, Scalawag +2. These bonuses are applied to the d6 of a Challenge roll.

Say that this trader is attempting to talk a town guard into letting him through. She makes a Challenge roll and gets 4/6-5/6. With the bonus, however, this becomes 7/6-8/6, turning the failure into a success.


Expertise
Characters have two areas of Expertise when they begin. There are martial and non-martial areas of expertise, and players must choose one of each. More types of expertise can be developed with the expenditure of XP and a month of training thereafter.

Health
All characters begin with 5 hit points (HP), an abstract indicator of a person's overall condition. When a character gains a level, the player may choose to increase their health by 1 HP or gain an additional Soul point. If a character loses all of their HP, they are not necessarily dead, merely too wounded or ill to continue fighting; to determine what their actual condition is, the owner of the character must make an unmodified Challenge roll after combat, or whatever event has harmed the character, has ceased. If they have died, a spell such as Prevent Death can still be used immediately to revive them.

Morale

Morale is determined by a character's Soul.
