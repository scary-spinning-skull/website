SOUL POINTS

Soul is an abstraction of a character's courage and their ability to "push through" and succeed despite the odds. Mechanically, Soul points are used in several ways"


  • Modifying the outcome of a Story die roll
  • Giving the character access to higher tiers of spells
  • Determining the ability of a character to overcome his fear

First, any Soul point can be spent on increasing the outcome of a one Story die roll of the player's choice, but not both. For example, a player announces that, given his character's +1 to Adept rolls, he is going to use one Soul point on his lowest roll to improve the outcome of a jump across a chasm. Including the +1 bonus, he rolls 2/5 (fail) and 7/8 (fail). The spent Soul point goes to the 4/7 roll and the rat falls to his death. If, however, he had rolled a 6/5 (pass) and 7/8 (fail) resulting in a Complication, his first roll would be raised to 7/5 and there would be no difference in the outcome. The player can also announce that he will give up one Soul point in anticipation of a marginal failure (3/4, for example), but if both rolls are a success, or the failures are too low for it to matter, the point is still lost.


Second, spending Soul points is necessary to access higher level spells. Level 1 spells are always available to characters, but the spending of a Soul point is necessary if a character is to use level 2 spells, and two are needed to use level 3 spells. These spent points to not go towards affecting the outcome of Story die rolls, but they are not counted when calculating the harmful effects ot a miscast spell, either (more on that in the SPELLS section).


Finally, Soul points are used when determinging a character's reaction to frightful circumstances, specifically encounters with Giants and encounters in which half of a party have perished. To do this, the current Soul score is doubled, then checked against a d10 roll. If the result of that roll is less than the Soul score, the character's courage holds. If that roll is greater, their courage breaks and they flee. On a tie, the player may decide if the character remains frozen with fear, giving them another turn to muster their courage, or if they run away.


For example, say that a Mischief of three rats turns a corner in an alley and encounters a hungry Cat. Rat 1 has a current Soul score of 3, meaning that he has to roll below 6 with a d10 to keep his wits about him and deploy the can of mace he has strapped to his back. He succeeds: 6/4. Rat 2 is emotionally exhausted because he cast a very important spell earlier in the session, and his Soul has been reduced to 1. He fails his roll dramatically: 2/7. He runs away, while Rat 3, with 4 Soul points, rolls 8/8 and is rooted to the ground. On the next turn he rolls 8/4, allowing him to gather his courage and join Rat 1 in his fight with the blinded cat.
